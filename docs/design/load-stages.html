Load Stages — traffic that changes over time, one step at a time

The owner chose a stages table — the k6/Gatling grammar — over a drawn curve, and set the rule: uwspólniać co jest, upraszczać, nie dodawać kolejnych elementów jeśli się da — consolidate what exists, simplify, add nothing new that a seam already carries. This revision locks in the owner's now-ratified decisions and adds the two designs they turn on: multi-cycle superposition (a daily shape × a seasonal shape on one stream) and the two-tier evaluation that makes a multi-cycle span computable at all. The decisions: the level anchor is the baseline (k6 semantics; cost still reads the mean so the bill stays honest); cycles multiply within a generator, sum across generators (the additive case is just another origin node — no toggle); everything is live — all cycles always evaluated, no play button, an ambient result. The feature stays a net removal: the global stress probe is deleted, one shape representation replaces two, one level convention replaces two, and the transient run becomes ambient rather than an action.

About this document & the project (context for the outside reader).

SDA (System Design Assistant) computes an architecture drawn on a canvas — load, latency, availability, cost — against declared requirements, live on every edit. A traffic origin declares how much load it offers; today that is one sustained number, plus (from the load-curves feature) an optional 24-hour shape. The owner reviewed load-curves' drawn-chart editor and picked the alternative it weighed: a k6/Gatling-style stages table — an ordered list of steps, each a duration and a relative multiplier — because a table is exact, keyboard-native, diffable, and reads like the load-testing tools architects already use. He attached a hard constraint: the feature must consolidate, not accrete — remove and unify existing mechanisms rather than lay a second set beside them. This revision records the decisions he has since ratified and designs the two mechanisms they enable — stacking several periodic shapes on one stream, and evaluating a span too long for brute-force simulation. It is honest that it replaces load-curves' curve?: {points} shape (nothing shipped carries one — §12). Definitions, stated positively:

generatora port function generate(level, …) — the sixth member of the closed flow-function family (ratio / batch / cap / window / prob), already shipped (load-curves R1, engine/core/src/graph.ts). The five siblings reshape traffic crossing a port; a generator originates it. It is the general way a node makes traffic — not a spike tool — symmetric with the transforms: some nodes generate, some transform, most relay.
stageone step of a schedule: over its durationS seconds the generator's rate ramps linearly to multiplier × its baseline. Reaching a target and holding it is a stage whose multiplier equals the previous one; a hard step is a stage of near-zero duration.
cycle this revisiona periodic shape: an ordered stages list that repeats every periodS seconds. One generator carries a list of cycles (a daily cycle, a weekly cycle, a quarterly cycle); within a generator they multiply into one shaped stream (§5). Empty = a flat generator, byte-identical to today's steady generate(level).
level vs shapethe level is the generator's baseline rate — the ×1 the multipliers are relative to (the ratified k6 reading), and the number worlds and Monte-Carlo already manipulate (assumedRps). The shape is the cycles — how the rate departs from that baseline over time. Rate at instant t = level × Πcycles multiplier(t). The mean and peak are DERIVED from the shape (the mean bills, the peak judges — §7).
product within / sum acrossseveral cycles on one generator MULTIPLY — a daily curve × a quarterly curve is one stream, shaped twice (§5). Several generators / origin nodes SUM — and that already exists (offered demand is inflow + Σ self(assumedRps); two origins superpose at a target's inflow). So an additive workload — a quarterly batch report — is modelled as its own generator node, summed by the engine: no sum/product toggle, no additive flag.
ambient transient runa time-bounded simulation that plays the declared cycles and reports, per fixed window, the sojourn p50/p99, arrivals/served/failed, retry amplification, and each station's backlog over time — plus peak backlog and drain time. Already built (engine/sim/src/transient.ts). This document re-aims it from a global spike to per-node cycles and makes it ambient — always computed, never a button (§8, §10).
drain timeseconds from when a disturbance ends until every station is back within its pre-disturbance backlog and tail — the honest measure of "did it recover, and how fast". null when recovery was not observed.

1The workflow this serves — a table the architect can type, on the node that actually spikes

Two owner decisions shaped the original document; a third round of decisions, now ratified, shapes this one. First, on the form: load-curves offered a drawn chart or a stages table, and the owner chose the table. Second, on the scope: a spike belongs to one generator, not to the whole design. Third, on evaluation: everything is live, several periodic shapes stack on one stream, and the level's anchor is the baseline.

The form decision is a preference for exactness over gesture. A drawn curve is expressive but imprecise — you cannot type "×5 for exactly 120 seconds", you cannot diff two curves in an export, and a screen-reader cannot narrate a dragged point. A stages table is what k6, Gatling and JMeter converged on precisely because a capacity plan is written, reviewed and version-controlled: [hold ×1 for 60 s, ramp to ×5 over 10 s, hold ×5 for 120 s, ramp to ×1 over 10 s] is unambiguous, keyboard-editable, and a one-line change in a review. SDA is a planning tool, not a finger-painting canvas; the table is the honest instrument.

The scope decision is the deeper correction. The stress probe SDA ships today multiplies every traffic origin by the same factor at once — but no real incident does that. A flash sale hits the checkout path; a retry storm hits one dependency; a cron burst hits the service that hosts the cron. The neighbours stay flat. A per-origin schedule is the only honest model of a real transient, and it is exactly what "the shape lives on the generator" already implies — a spike is just a generator whose cycles are non-flat. This document therefore generalises: generate() is the one way a node originates traffic, flat or shaped; cycles describe its shape; the design plays whatever each node declares, live. The global probe is not kept beside this — it is deleted into it (§2, §8).

The evaluation decisions close the loop the owner opened when he asked for both a launch spike and an operating day, and later for "wszystko powinno być na żywo" — everything on live. A generator can carry more than one periodic shape (a daily rhythm and a quarterly report season); they compose by multiplication into one stream (§5). All declared shapes are always evaluated together — there is no "play transient" action, only an ambient result that refreshes as the design rests (§8, §10). And because a real multi-cycle span is far too long to simulate arrival-by-arrival, the ambient result is produced by a two-tier evaluation — a cheap analytic sweep over the whole span, and a targeted simulation on the one window that matters (§10).

3Research — the industry writes load as a table of stages

load-curves surveyed the same field for the drawn-curve question and found the answer was never a spline. Re-read for the table question, the consensus is even sharper: every load tool expresses time-varying traffic as an ordered list of steps, and the two most-used ones make each step a (duration, target) pair with a linear ramp between targets — which is exactly the shape this document adopts, so SDA invents no grammar of its own.

The distilled grammar SDA adopts — and the two deliberate SDA twists.

The field agrees on a list of (duration, target) steps with a linear ramp to each target; a hold is a target equal to the previous one, a hard step is a zero-duration ramp. SDA takes that grammar unchanged. It twists it in exactly two places, for reasons load-curves already argued: the target is a relative multiplier of the generator's baseline level, not an absolute rate (the level already lives in assumedRps — an absolute target would be a second place to declare the same fact, and Azure's spikeMultiplier shows the relative form is a real idiom); and a stages list is wrapped in a cycle with a periodS, because a design describes both transient events (a launch) and operating rhythms (a diurnal shape, a quarterly season), where every load tool only ever runs one-shot — and SDA can stack several of those rhythms on one stream (§5), which no load tool does.

This is not a reversal of load-curves — it is the same relative-and-periodic stance, delivered through the grammar the tools standardised on rather than a drawn line. Where load-curves lowered a drawn curve to a piecewise-linear rate function, a cycle's stages are that piecewise-linear function, written directly — so the lowering code (sim.ts:84) is reused, only renamed.

4The model — cycles ride the generator; each step is a relative multiplier off the baseline

A generator already exists as the sixth port function (engine/core/src/graph.ts — verified). This document swaps its optional drawn curve for an optional list of cycles and a live disable flag. Each cycle is an ordered stages list that repeats every periodS. The multiplier is relative to the one declared level — which is the baseline (ratified); the shape owns no rate.

/** One periodic shaping cycle: its `stages` describe a departure from the ×1 baseline over [0, Σ durationS],
 *  then hold ×1 to `periodS` and WRAP. Several cycles on one generator MULTIPLY (a daily curve × a quarterly
 *  curve = one stream shaped twice — §5). A one-shot launch spike is a cycle whose `periodS` exceeds the
 *  observed span, so it plays once. `×1` is the BASELINE (= the level); the mean and peak are DERIVED. */
export interface Cycle {
  readonly periodS: number;             // > 0; the tiling period. Σ stages.durationS <= periodS (baseline tail)
  readonly stages: readonly Stage[];    // the k6/Gatling schedule that repeats every periodS
}

/** One k6/Gatling-style stage: over `durationS` seconds the generator's rate ramps LINEARLY to
 *  `multiplier` × its baseline. A hold repeats the previous multiplier; a hard step uses a near-zero
 *  duration. `×1` is the baseline (= the level). REPLACES LoadCurve/LoadCurvePoint — deleted; there is one
 *  shape atom, not two. */
export interface Stage {
  readonly durationS: number;   // > 0 (a hard step is the shortest representable ramp — see §9)
  readonly multiplier: number;  // >= 0, the TARGET reached at the END of this stage (×1 = the baseline)
}

5Multi-cycle superposition — product within a generator, sum across generators ratified

A real stream is shaped by more than one rhythm at once: a daily peak and a quarterly season. The owner ratified the composition rule — cycles on one generator multiply; distinct workloads on separate origin nodes sum. The sum half already exists; only the product is new, and it is one function, not a toggle.

5.1 Product within a generator — one stream, shaped twice

A generator's cycles compose by multiplication: the instantaneous multiplier is the product of every cycle's multiplier at that instant, each read at its own phase.

λ(t) = level · Πc ∈ cycles  mc( t mod periodSc )

This is the honest model of a daily rhythm running inside a slow season: on an ordinary day the daily curve swings ×0.5…×1.5 around a ×1 quarter; during the report week the quarterly curve lifts to ×3, so the same daily peak now reaches ×4.5 — the two peaks superimpose exactly where they coincide. Multiplication (not addition) is what makes "the busy hour of the busy week" the worst instant, which is the physical truth a capacity plan must see. Because ×1 is the baseline for every cycle, a flat cycle contributes a factor of 1 and vanishes — adding a cycle can only reshape, never silently rescale, the stream.

5.2 Sum across generators — the additive workload is its own node (already exists)

An additive distinct workload — a quarterly batch report job that lands on top of the interactive traffic rather than shaping it — is not a cycle at all. It is a separate origin. SDA already sums arrivals across origins, so the batch job is modelled as its own generator node, and the engine superposes it with the existing stream for free:

Honest limit — the product of piecewise-linear cycles is piecewise-quadratic, not linear.

The NHPP inversion sampler (profile.ts:137) is exact only for a piecewise-linear rate. A single cycle is linear and lowers to a RateProfile byte-for-byte; the product of two linear cycles is not, so there is no single exact global profile for a multi-cycle stream over a long span. This is precisely why the evaluation is two-tier (§10): the Tier-1 sweep needs only the scalar λ(t) at each window's centre — a trivial product of a handful of numbers, exact and cheap — and the Tier-2 DES runs on a window narrow enough that a fine local linear approximation of the product is accurate (over a 15-minute window a daily×quarterly product is nearly constant). The flat identity is untouched: no cycles ⇒ a flat profile ⇒ today's exponential sampling, bit-for-bit.

7Semantics — cost reads the mean, verdicts read the peak, everything is live

The ratified anchor collapses the old regime split. ×1 is the baseline; from the shape two figures are derived — the mean (which bills) and the peak (which judges). There is no "one-shot ignored by steady" branch anymore: every declared cycle is always evaluated, and the honest cost is the integral, not the spike.

Which SLO judges a played event (inherited from load-curves tension 7).

Capacity and utilisation verdicts read the peak window — a promise broken for a minute a day is broken. Percentile SLOs are judged on the worst window's tail and also reported pooled over the played span, both shown and named for what they are (p99 · worst window / p99 · whole run) — a p99 pooled over a quiet season would drown the spike, and a p99 of only the worst window judges an instant, not the promise. The windowed strip is the diagnosis surface; the verdict never averages a violation away.

10The two-tier evaluation — the sweep proposes, the DES proves the hard-engine answer

This is the design's load-bearing new section. A multi-cycle span is far too long to simulate arrival-by-arrival, so brute-force DES is not an option and we must not pretend otherwise. The answer is two labelled tiers: a cheap analytic time-sweep over the whole span that finds where and how badly the design strains, and a targeted DES on the single worst window that proves the true backlog and drain. "Propose (cheap) / prove (exact)", applied to time — and it maps onto the resting handshake the app already runs.

10.1 Why brute force is off the table — the arithmetic, stated honestly

Take a modest multi-cycle design: a daily rhythm inside a 90-day quarterly season, baseline ≈ 1000 req/s. To observe the quarterly superposition you must span the quarter:

90 days × 86 400 s/day × 1000 req/s  ≈  7.78 × 10⁹ arrivals  (events strictly more: ×(1 + station visits))
                          vs  TRANSIENT_EVENT_CAP = 5 000 000   (engine/sim/src/transient.ts:52)
                              7.78 × 10⁹ / 5 × 10⁶  ≈  1 555×  over the cap  — ~1500× infeasible

The transient runner would (honestly) truncate at ~0.06% of the span (truncated: true, transient.ts:108–110) — it would never reach the report week. Simulating a quarter of arrivals to find one bad afternoon is the wrong tool. The right tool evaluates the steady response at many instants cheaply, then simulates only the instant that matters.

10.2 Tier 1 — the analytic time-sweep (quasi-static)

Partition the auto-derived span (§10.4) into windows (default 15 min). At each window's instantaneous rate λ(t) = level · Π cycles(t) — a scalar product of a handful of numbers — evaluate the steady-state M/M/c response of every node via the analytic twin the hot path already runs (content/sda/src/queueing.ts: NodeQueue with ρ and Erlang-C sojourn via mmc). This is exactly the EvaluateBatch seam — "the SAME design under many input scenarios in one call" (engine/solver-contract/src/capability/evaluate-batch.ts), each window a scenario whose override is the instantaneous λ. It yields, over the whole span:

Tier-1 outputHow
ρ envelope over timeper node, ρ(t) = λ(t)/(c·μ) at each window — the utilisation film across the season.
WHERE cycles superimposethe worst window = argmax ρ(t) (per node, and system-wide) — the daily peak inside the quarterly peak (§5, Fig. 2). This is the window Tier 2 zooms.
Cost integralthe honest bill from the mean over the span (§7) — level × mean(Π cycles), not the spike.
% of windows in violationthe fraction of the span where a capacity/SLO band is exceeded — "the design is over budget 4% of the quarter, concentrated in the report week".

Feasibility: a 90-day span at 15-min windows = 90 × 24 × 4 ≈ 8 640 windows; one M/M/c evaluation per node per window is sub-millisecond on the hot path, so a whole quarter sweeps in seconds on the CPU — and the EvaluateBatch seam is explicitly GPU-batchable (TASK-79 "GPU proposes"), taking it to milliseconds.

Honest limit — quasi-static assumes equilibrium is reached fast relative to the window.

Tier 1 reads the steady-state response at each window's rate, which is accurate only when the system relaxes to equilibrium well within the window — i.e. when service time ≪ window width. It therefore captures the ρ envelope, the superposition point and the cost integral, but it does not capture a multi-hour transient build-up (a queue that never drains between windows because each window inherits the last one's backlog). That is precisely what Tier 2 exists to measure. The basis is labelled analytic (quasi-static) on every Tier-1 figure — never presented as measured dynamics.

10.3 Tier 2 — the targeted DES (measured transient)

On the one worst window Tier 1 found, run the real DES — the untouched transient.ts windowed metrics plus the survival verdict, exactly as §8 re-homes them — over a neighbourhood of that window (± a bounded margin, sized to fit the 5M-event cap: hours at a few-hundred-rps baseline, shrinking to minutes at a multi-thousand-rps peak, and truncating loudly if the cap binds). This buys the true backlog build-up, drain time and tail at the stack point — the dynamics Tier 1 cannot see. The basis is labelled measured (transient).

Propose / prove, applied to time — and it is the resting handshake, not a new scheduler. Tier 1 is the cheap proposal (where does it strain?); Tier 2 is the expensive proof (what actually happens there?). Two labelled bases — analytic (quasi-static) and measured (transient) — never blurred, so the must-not-lie discipline holds. And the live/rest split is already in the app: the ambient-uncertainty loop runs a fast preview while editing and a confirmed pass when the design rests (app/web/src/app.tsx:120–123, TASK-81; the ideal-layout polisher does the same, :285–290). The two-tier run rides that same handshake — the Tier-1 sweep is the live ambient read-out; the Tier-2 DES refine fires when the design rests. No play button, no new scheduling primitive.

10.4 The auto-derived observation span — no "observe N days" slider

The engine derives the span from the declared cycles, so the user never sets a horizon. The span is the slowest cycle's period × a few repeats (enough to show the season recurring), sampled at a resolution fine enough to resolve BOTH the fastest cycle and the shortest stage feature (the window must be ≪ the fastest periodS and ≤ the shortest ramp/hold ÷ 4, so a brief spike inside a long period is not skipped; and — for Tier 1's validity — ≫ the service time). A daily×quarterly design therefore spans a few quarters at ~15-min windows; a purely diurnal design spans a few days at the same resolution; a one-shot spike is a SHORT cycle (its period is its own duration), so it spans a few multiples of the shape and the spike is fully resolved. When a fine feature and a long span cannot both fit the window budget, the span shrinks (fewer periods) before the window coarsens — a declared spike is never silently averaged away. The residual choices (how many repeats; the exact window default; the live-vs-rest resolution split) are sourced and named in §16.2–16.3 — each a documented constant in LOAD_STAGES_DEFAULTS, taken from Prometheus/Datadog/Grafana (window), AWS predictive scaling (span) and Welch's method (DES neighbourhood).

The one-form rule & a lint to pin it.

Every surface that needs one of these numbers reads LOAD_STAGES_DEFAULTS — the sweep's window sizing, the span derivation, the DES neighbourhood, the editor's live preview, the generated doc's Transient section. None carries a local literal. A follow-up scoped no-magic-numbers lint (an ESLint no-magic-numbers rule for this family, or a unit test asserting the sweep/DES import the constant rather than inlining 96/300/2) pins it so a future edit cannot re-scatter them. Same discipline the codebase already applies to TARGET_UTILIZATION and STRESS_DEFAULTS.

The ledger stays honest — a constants module is config, not a mechanism. Naming numbers adds no moving part: it does not change what is computed, only where the numbers are declared. It absorbs STRESS_DEFAULTS (one existing constant folded in, not a second added), so against the net-negative ledger (§2) it is at worst neutral and arguably −1. What it buys is the owner's requirement in full: every default is now sourced from the best-in-class tool for that decision, and changeable in one line.
