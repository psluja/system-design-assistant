Load Curves — the day as an input

Traffic is generated by a port function: generate(level, curve) joins the flow-transform family, and the 24-hour multiplier curve — pure shape, editable as a chart — rides the generator. The scalar pass judges the worst hour and bills the mean; the analytic sweep films the day; the DES plays λ(t) with true tails. One assumption space, extended by time.
SDA design document · 2026-07-05 · status: for owner review — research + design, no code
About this document & the project (context for the outside reader).

SDA (System Design Assistant) computes an architecture drawn on a canvas — load, latency, availability, cost — against declared requirements. Today every traffic origin declares its offered load as one sustained number (assumedRps); every verdict, bill and simulation reads a world where the day is perfectly flat. This document adds the missing axis — time of day — as an owner-commissioned feature, with an industry-research section and an evaluated UI recommendation. Definitions used throughout, stated positively:

generatora port function generate(level, curve?) — the sixth member of the closed flow-function family (ratio / batch / cap / window / prob) that already sits on ports. Where the five existing functions reshape traffic flowing through a port, a generator originates it: the port emits a stream of its own.
load curvea periodic, piecewise-linear function of time-of-day attached to one generator; its value is a relative multiplier of the generator's level — the shape of the day, owning no req/s of its own.
level vs shapethe level is the generator's rate — the day's mean req/s, the number worlds and Monte-Carlo already manipulate (today's assumedRps, which becomes sugar for a generator). The shape is the curve — how that mean distributes across the hours. Rate at any instant = level × shape(t).
peak factor kthe curve's maximum effective multiplier — how many times busier the worst instant is than the day's mean. A flat day has k = 1.
windowone slice of the day (15 min or 1 h) evaluated as if its rate held steady — the unit of the analytic day-sweep and of the DES's windowed metrics.
NHPPnon-homogeneous Poisson process — random arrivals whose rate λ(t) varies over time; the mathematically standard model of a day of traffic, and what the simulator generates when a curve is present.
quasi-statictreating a slowly-changing rate as a sequence of steady states — valid when the system settles much faster than the curve moves (hours vs seconds), which is exactly the daily-curve regime outside sharp spikes.

1The workflow this serves — one number flattens the day

No real system receives its mean rate all day. Traffic breathes: business hours, an evening peak, a weekend lull, a flash sale. A single assumedRps forces the architect to pick a lie: size to the mean and break every evening, or size to the peak and read a monthly bill inflated three-fold.

Both errors are silent today. Capacity verdicts judged at the mean ride green while the 19:00 peak would saturate the pool; pay-per-use cost computed from a peak-set level overstates the bill exactly by the peak factor. The two numbers the architect actually needs — what the worst hour does to my promises and what the whole day costs — come from two different points of the same curve, and the tool currently has no curve to read them from.

The owner's commission, in two parts. First: every traffic origin carries a relative time function — a 24-hour multiplier curve, editable as a chart — that the DES interprets. Second, the widening that sets the model's home: traffic generation is a port-level function in the same closed family as the flow transforms — instead of reshaping traffic, a port function can generate it, and the time function lives where functions live: on the generator. This also dissolves a recorded limitation — a node may now relay on some ports and generate on others (a saga emitting timer-driven follow-ups, a cron job on a shared service, a health-checker inside an app) instead of being forced to be either a relay or an origin. This document designs the feature end-to-end: the generator model, the curve, the scalar semantics, the analytic day-sweep, the simulator changes, the composition with worlds / Monte-Carlo / request classes, and the curve editor — grounded in what the industry does and in the verified seams of the existing code.

The distilled common grammar — and where SDA deliberately differs.

Everyone converged on the same shape language: an ordered list of segments (duration, target-level) with linear interpolation between targets. Nothing in the field uses splines or formulas; piecewise-linear is the industry's curve. Two axes split the field: values are overwhelmingly absolute (VUs or RPS — the only relative multipliers live in Azure's spikeMultiplier and in the autoscaling world, where the daily pattern itself is the object), and load tests are one-shot while periodicity lives only in autoscaling (cron recurrence, learned diurnal cycles).

SDA adopts the consensus where it is real consensus — piecewise-linear control points — and takes the autoscaling side of both splits: the curve is relative (the level already lives in assumedRps and is already the thing worlds, Monte-Carlo and the envelope manipulate — an absolute curve would create a second place to state the same fact) and periodic (a design document describes an operating day, not a test run). SDA is a capacity-planning tool, not a load generator; its natural relatives are the diurnal curves autoscaling learns, expressed in the grammar the test tools standardised.

Arrival-process mathematics: simulating a time-varying Poisson stream is a solved problem with two standard algorithms — thinning (P. A. W. Lewis & G. S. Shedler, "Simulation of nonhomogeneous Poisson processes by thinning", Naval Research Logistics Quarterly 26(3), 1979; open copy at calhoun.nps.edu): generate candidates at a majorizing rate λ* ≥ λ(t) and accept each with probability λ(t)/λ* — and inversion (time-change through the inverse of the cumulative rate Λ; Poisson point process, simulation; canonical text: Çinlar, Introduction to Stochastic Processes, 1975). For piecewise-linear λ(t), Λ is piecewise-quadratic with a closed-form inverse, so inversion is exact, rejection-free and draws exactly one uniform per arrival. §6 picks between them on determinism grounds.

3The model — generate() at a port; the curve is pure shape

Traffic generation joins the closed port-function family. Where ratio / batch / cap / window / prob reshape the flow crossing a port, generate(level, curve?) originates flow at one — and the time function lives where functions live: on the generator. The curve multiplies; it never owns a rate. The generator's level is the single place the rate is declared — and it is, by construction, the day's mean.

// engine/core/src/graph.ts — the closed union grows by one member (pure math, domain-free):
export type Transform =
  | { kind: 'ratio';    value: number }
  | { kind: 'batch';    value: number }
  | { kind: 'cap';      value: number }
  | { kind: 'window';   value: number }
  | { kind: 'prob';     value: number }
  | { kind: 'generate'; level: number; curve?: LoadCurve };   // NEW: out = through-flow + served(level)

/** A periodic, piecewise-linear multiplier over a declared period (default 24 h). Pure shape:
 *  the engine reads it normalized so its mean over the period is exactly 1. */
interface LoadCurve {
  readonly periodHours?: number;                 // default 24; the only period v1 ships
  readonly points: readonly { readonly t: number; readonly m: number }[]; // t in hours ∈ [0, period), m ≥ 0
}
PieceDesign
The family it joinsthe closed Transform union in engine/core/src/graph.ts — a rate and a shape are pure math (like Distribution), so the engine stays domain-free. The scalar arithmetic composes with the family: a generator port's emission is its through-flow (shaped by nothing — generate replaces the reshaping slot) plus the generator's served share; a port with no through-flow (the common source port) emits exactly the served level. The universal overflow keeps its one form: max(0, inflow + Σ levels − capacity).
Where a generator liveseverywhere the family already resolves, with the same precedence (verified: content/sda/src/sim.ts and flow-transformations-r2 §5 — wire > instance port > manifest port): a catalog block may ship one (a cron component's out port generates by nature), an instance may declare/override one (Instance.transforms — the existing port-name-keyed container, unchanged), and a wire-level generator injects into one specific wire's emission. A node may relay on some ports and generate on others — the recorded relay-vs-origin limitation is gone structurally.
assumedRps becomes sugara node-level assumedRps declaration compiles to a generator on the node's primary out port (its first out/bi port). deserialize maps it on load, extending the legacy demand-key chain (originRps → demandRps → assumedRps, app/core/src/document.ts) by one final link — kept forever, idempotent, and behavior-preserving: the generator physics is a superset of today's origin physics (see the callout below), so a migrated document evaluates identically. A legacy source client's throughput-as-workload maps the same way.
One address for worlds / MCthe compiled graph keeps the node's assumedRps input cell as the reconciled total of its generators' levels — exactly the originByNode precedent from request classes (per-port declarations authoritative, the class-blind total supplied where the shared relations read it). Scenario overrides, Monte-Carlo ranges and the envelope keep addressing node|assumedRps untouched; on a multi-generator node an override scales the ports proportionally (v1 norm: one generator per node — open question 8 ratifies the rule).
The curve rides the generatoran optional field of generate, nowhere else — time-functions live where functions live. Absent ⇒ a flat day, and the whole curve feature is silent (no-filler); a design with no generator evaluates bit-for-bit as today (sugar compiles to the same physics).
Normalize-at-readThe stored points are exactly what the architect drew. The engine reads the effective multiplier ŝ(t) = m(t) / m̄ where m̄ is the drawn curve's mean over the period — so the effective shape has mean 1 by construction, always. The alternative (rewriting the stored points to mean 1 on every edit) makes the whole curve visibly shift each time one point is dragged — the editor would fight the hand. Normalize-at-read keeps stored = drawn and model = pure shape; there is no state in which level and shape double-count. Peak factor k = max(m)/m̄.
Role disciplinethe generator's level is a fact-assumption exactly as assumedRps is today (role in content/sda/src/registry.ts, polarity higher-is-worse): scenario-overridable, MC-rangeable — through the reconciled cell, so the role machinery is untouched. The shape is a fact-assumption by the same declaration (a belief about the world's traffic); its scalar shadow — the peak factor k — is what the numeric passes consume. Neither is a new registry key: generate is a port function resolved like its five siblings.
Validationon load and on edit, guided errors (the ranges discipline): level ≥ 0; points sorted by t, unique t within [0, period), every m ≥ 0, and m̄ > 0 (an all-zero curve makes "the mean" meaningless — refused naming the fix, never a silent divide-by-zero). One point is legal and means a flat day. A generate on an in-port is refused naming the port's direction.
Presets as datafive named shapes shipped as content constants (the DERIVED_DEMAND_FRACTIONS precedent — tunable in one place, pure data): flat · business-hours (9–17 plateau, quiet night) · evening-peak (the consumer curve of fig. 1) · weekend-day (broad late-morning-to-evening hump) · flash-sale (flat base + one sharp spike). A preset is a starting shape — fully editable after insertion, never a mode.
Serializationschema 7 → 8, additive like every bump before it (app/core/src/document.ts): generate rides in the very containers the family already serializes (manifest port transform, Instance.transforms, the wire transform) — a new union member, no new field. A schema ≤7 export has no generator anywhere; the sugar mapping gives it identical semantics on load, and a schema-8 file with no generators round-trips byte-identically. Plain arrays — no Map handling.
Injection vs emission — the semantics tension, resolved explicitly.

Does generated traffic consume the generating node's own service capacity before exiting the declared port, or does it materialize on the wire for free? Resolved: it consumes — a cron job eats its host. The generator's offered level enters the node's own offered load (service time, concurrency, the universal overflow), and its served share exits the declared port. This is a superset of today's physics, not a change: a pure origin already emits min(capacity, assumedRps) and overflows at max(0, inflow + assumedRps − capacity) (verified: content/sda/src/registry.ts, the withOrigin wrapper) — so every migrated declaration evaluates identically, while the new mixed case (a service that relays and hosts a nightly batch) finally charges the batch to the host that runs it.

The alternative — pure emission (the stream costs the emitter nothing) — is documented and rejected for v1: an external pusher that does no local work is already modeled honestly by a dedicated zero-service source node (exactly what client blocks are today — zero-service passthrough stations in the DES). Pure emission would only matter if one node had to both do real work and host a cost-free foreign stream on the same box — no named case demands it; revisit with one, as an additive free: true flag if ever.

4Scalar semantics — judge the worst hour, bill the mean

The sustained-vs-peak question dissolves once level = mean and shape = curve: every consumer reads the point of the day it honestly describes.

QuantityEvaluated atWhy this is the honest point
Capacity, utilization ρ, queueing latency, overflow — every verdictpeak: level × kThe day breaks at its worst hour. A verdict green at the mean and red at 19:00 is a broken promise; "real by default" (doc-11) demands the tool lead with the hour that bites. Verdicts are badged at peak (×2.3) so the provenance is visible.
Pay-per-use cost, egressmean: level × 1A bill integrates the day. Because the level IS the mean, every existing cost relation is already correct, unchanged and to the letter: payPerUseCost = inflow(throughput) × unitCost and the egress relation's × MONTH_SECONDS (content/sda/src/behaviors.ts) multiply a sustained mean into a month — exactly what the level now guarantees to be.
Capacity envelopeboth edgesThe envelope (content/sda/src/envelope.ts) reports per-origin maxRps; with a curve it reports headroom twice — mean headroom (maxRps / level) and peak headroom (maxRps / (level × k)) — and the day's first-breaking hour when peak demand crosses the edge.
Mechanism: the peak pass is a derived world — zero new solver machinery.

The scalar peak evaluation is literally a scenario: override each curved origin's reconciled level cell (node|assumedRps, §3) to level × korigin and evaluate. That is one more entry in the ambient EvaluateBatch that already carries the base world, the named worlds and Monte-Carlo (content/sda/src/scenario.ts, uncertainty.ts — the same "node|key" addressing onto fixed input cells). The batch becomes [mean, peak, …worlds]; verdicts read from the peak entry, cost and the register from the mean entry. The engine contract stays scalar and domain-agnostic — the curve never crosses the engine boundary in the scalar pass. No curve anywhere, or a flat curve (k = 1) ⇒ the peak entry is skipped and everything is today, bit-for-bit.

5The day as a batch — the analytic sweep

Between "two points" (§4) and "a full simulation" (§6) sits the cheap film of the whole day: evaluate every window of the period as its own steady state. The machinery already exists — it is one more EvaluateBatch.

The sweep lowers the day to 96 scenarios (15-minute windows; each overrides every curved origin to level × ŝ(tw)) and reads, per window: every node's ρ, the queueing-aware latency, served throughput and overflow. A thousand-scenario batch is routine for Monte-Carlo today, so 96 is ambient-cheap — the sweep can re-run on every edit, like everything else. On top of the per-window steady states, two fluid quantities capture what steady state cannot:

QuantityComputationReads as
Backlog build-upper node, the fluid recursion Bw+1 = max(0, Bw + (λw − μ) · Δ) across the day's windows (λw = the window's offered rate, μ = the node's capacity)"the 19:00 hour banks ≈170 k requests of queue"
Drain timefirst window after the peak where the fluid backlog returns to ~0"the backlog drains by ≈21:10 — 70 min after the peak passes"

This quasi-static treatment is exact in the model wherever the curve moves in hours and the system settles in seconds — the whole day except sharp spikes, which is precisely where the DES takes over. The sweep is also what the generated design document renders as the day strip (§8).

6The DES plays λ(t) — arrivals, windows, budget

The simulator is where the curve earns its keep: true percentiles inside the peak, the transient the sweep's fluid model only sketches. Three verified seams change — and nothing else.

6.1 What the code does today (verified)

SeamAs built
Arrival generationengine/sim/src/des.ts lines 509–523: each source seeds one arrival, and every arrival schedules the next by drawing sample(src.interarrival, rng) — a stationary renewal stream. The projector (content/sda/src/sim.ts line 132) hands it { kind: 'exponential', rate: emittedRps }: homogeneous Poisson at the level.
Run shapeSimOptions is completion-bounded (warmupCompletions + measureCompletions) — a steady-state design: warm up, then measure one long stationary window; percentiles come from run-wide reservoirs. There is no notion of wall-clock windows.
Ambient policythe web shell runs the DES in a worker, debounced 450 ms after each edit, at seed 7 with 10 k warm-up + 50 k measured completions (app/web/src/sim-worker.ts, app.tsx) — roughly 60 k system completions per ambient run.

6.2 The three changes

ChangeDesign
1 · NHPP arrivals by inversionone ArrivalSource per generator (not per node), each with an optional, domain-free rateProfile (piecewise-linear rate points over a period — pure timing data, like Distribution; the engine stays free of domain strings). A generated arrival's onward routing is restricted to the wires of the port that generated it — the same job-tagged routing restriction the request-class machinery already introduced (a generator is, in effect, an implicit stream), so a cron port's jobs never leak down the relay ports. Generation is by inversion: draw E ~ Exp(1) — one uniform, exactly today's draw — and advance to the t where the integrated rate Λ grows by E; Λ is piecewise-quadratic with a closed-form per-segment inverse. Chosen over Lewis–Shedler thinning for three reasons: it is exact for our piecewise-linear curves; it draws exactly one uniform per arrival, so the RNG stream stays aligned and — decisively — a flat profile reduces to today's −ln(1−u)/rate literally, byte-for-byte, which makes "no curve ≡ today" a testable identity rather than a statistical claim; and it wastes nothing on peaky curves, where thinning at λ* = k·λ̄ rejects most of the night's candidates. Thinning remains the documented fallback for any future non-piecewise-linear profile.
2 · A time-bounded run modealongside today's completion-bounded mode: play [t₀, t₁) of the period. The run starts at the curve's minimum point (rotating the day so the empty-system start bias is smallest) and warms through one window before measuring. Absent a curve, the mode is never entered — the existing path is untouched.
3 · Windowed accumulatorsper window: a system-sojourn reservoir (per-window p50/p95/p99), arrivals/completions/drops (loss windows), and per-station peak queue length; per-node reservoirs only for the peak window (bounded memory — 96 windows × every node's reservoir is not paid for). Two derived headlines: peak backlog and drain time (first window after the peak where total in-system returns to its pre-peak band) — the DES twins of §5's fluid numbers, and the differential test between them.

6.3 Budget honesty — the day is expensive, and the tool must say so

A full day at the fig.-2 scale is ≈17 M arrivals (200 req/s × 86 400 s) before counting per-station events — two orders of magnitude past the ambient 60 k-completion budget. The tool never silently scales, samples or lies about this:

RunPolicy
Ambient (every edit)stays steady-state, at the peak level (level × k) — the worst hour, sustained; the same budget as today. The tail-latency verdict therefore judges the hour that bites, labeled worst hour (×2.3). With no curve this is exactly today's ambient run.
"Play the day" (explicit action)a button/tool with a declared event budget (~2 M events, seconds in the worker). The tool computes the expected event count up front; if the full period fits, it plays the whole day. If not, it offers the widest true-rate window the budget affords, centred on the peak (e.g. "the budget covers 18:00–21:00 — play it?") and says exactly what it refused and why. The whole-day film is §5's sweep, which is always available; the DES buys the true tail where the money is.

7Composition — one assumption space, extended by time

One form, no special cases: the rate an origin offers at instant t, in world w, in Monte-Carlo draw d, is level(o; w, d) × ŝo(t). Levels compose exactly as they already do; the shape multiplies afterwards, at the content layer.

AxisComposition
× Worlds (scenarios)a world overrides the level today, through the existing scalar override on node|assumedRps — the reconciled generator total (§3), untouched (Black-Friday world = 5× level, same shape). A world may additionally override the shape: AssumptionScenario gains an optional, additive curves: [{node, port, points}] — the same role boundary (isScenarioOverridable: shape attaches only where a generator sits), the same guided refusal otherwise. Because the scalar pass consumes only k, a world's curve override never touches the engine contract either: the world's peak entry just uses its k. This is also how one-shot events are modeled: a flash-sale day is not a new mechanism — it is a world whose curve override is the flash shape (§9, tension 5).
× Monte-Carlouncertainty ranges draw the level (they already ride on assumedRps); each drawn scenario is judged at its own peak, draw × k. The tornado and SLO-confidence machinery are unchanged — the curve adds no random dimension of its own in v1 (an uncertain shape would need evidence we don't have; the honest form of "maybe the peak is worse" is a range on the level or a named world with a peakier curve).
× Request classesa class origin is {node, rps} (content/sda/src/request-class.ts): rates per class, per node — and under declared classes the per-class origins stay authoritative, with the reconciled total supplied class-blind, exactly as today (originByNode). In v1 every class originating at a node inherits that node's generator curve — the common case (one product's users). Per-class shapes at one node (reads peak in the evening, batch writes at 03:00) are a real future: ClassOrigin gains an optional curve additively when a named case demands it — and the generator model already makes the natural home visible (a class's origin at a node IS a generation, so the two declarations converge rather than compete) (open question 4).
× Envelope / derived triothe envelope reports both edges (§4); the derived trio keeps sizing levels off the envelope exactly as today — its pessimistic world stresses the level, a peakier shape stays an authored world.

9Tensions — resolved here, for ratification

#TensionResolution
1Mean-normalize or not? If the curve is stored normalized, dragging one point rewrites every other (the editor fights the hand); if it is not normalized at all, level × curve double-counts and assumedRps stops meaning anything.Normalize at read, not at rest. Stored = drawn; the engine divides by the drawn mean, so the effective shape has mean 1 by construction and assumedRps stays THE level = the day's mean. Peak = max/mean. No editor jump, no double-counting state, cost formulas literally unchanged (§3, §4).
2Sustained vs peak — one scalar pass cannot honestly serve both the verdict (breaks at peak) and the bill (integrates the day).Don't pick — split by consumer. Verdicts at level × k via a derived peak entry in the existing batch; cost/egress at the mean (= the level, by tension 1); envelope reports both edges. Badge every peak-judged verdict at peak (×k) (§4).
3The DES cannot afford a real day. 200 req/s × 86 400 s ≈ 17 M arrivals — two orders past the ambient budget; silently scaling rates or sampling the day would be a lie about transients.Budget honesty, three tiers. Ambient = steady state at the peak level (worst hour, today's budget). The whole-day film = the analytic sweep (96-window batch — exact in the quasi-static regime). True transients = explicit "play the day" that states its event budget and, when the day doesn't fit, offers the widest true-rate window it affords, centred on the peak — refusing loudly, never scaling silently (§6.3).
4Determinism with a modulated Poisson stream. Piecewise-linear λ(t) has a closed-form integrated rate; inversion is exact, one-uniform-per-arrival, and reduces byte-for-byte to today's exponential sampling when the profile is flat — turning the compatibility gate into a testable identity. Thinning stays documented as the fallback for non-PWL profiles (§6.2).
5Period vs one-shot events. A daily curve is periodic; a flash sale or launch day is one specific day, not every day.A one-shot is a world, not a mechanism. The base curve describes the operating day; a flash-sale day is a named world whose additive curve override is the flash shape — judged, compared and priced through the existing world matrix. The period stays declared (default 24 h; only 24 h ships in v1) (§7).
6Injection vs emission — does generated traffic consume the generating node's own service capacity, or appear on the wire for free?It consumes — a cron eats its host (owner-resolved; the §3 callout carries the full argument). A superset of today's origin physics (min(capacity, level) served, universal overflow unchanged), so migrated documents evaluate identically; pure emission is documented and rejected — a cost-free external pusher is already a zero-service source node, and no named case requires both on one box.
7Which SLO judges a day with transient windows? A p99 pooled over the whole day is dominated by the quiet hours (the peak drowns statistically); a p99 of only the worst window judges an hour, not the promise.Capacity/utilization verdicts: the peak window — a promise broken for an hour a day is broken. Percentile SLOs: judged on the worst window's tail AND reported pooled over the played period, both shown, named for what they are (p99 · worst window / p99 · whole day). The strip (§5) is the diagnosis surface; the verdict never averages a violation away. Default ratification: open question 3.
